Introduction to the Harappan Civilization

The Harappan Civilization (also known as the Indus Valley Civilization) is one of the oldest urban civilizations in the world, dating back to approximately 3300 BCE - 1300 BCE.

Discovery: The civilization was first discovered at Harappa (Punjab, Pakistan) in 1921 by Daya Ram Sahni, and at Mohenjo-daro (Sindh, Pakistan) in 1922 by R.D. Banerjee.

It is a Bronze Age civilization that flourished in the northwestern regions of South Asia.

Knowledge Check #1

Which Harappan site is known for its ancient dockyard?
Lothal in Gujarat had an ancient dockyard, suggesting the Harappans had maritime trade connections. It is one of the most important archaeological sites of the civilization.

Town Planning

The Harappan cities displayed remarkable urban planning:

  • Grid Pattern: Streets were laid out in a grid pattern, intersecting at right angles
  • Two-part division: Cities had a Citadel (upper town) and Lower Town
  • Drainage System: Advanced covered drainage system - every house connected to street drains
  • Standardized Bricks: Ratio of 4:2:1 (length:width:height) used uniformly
  • Public Buildings: Great Bath, granaries, assembly halls
Remarkable: The drainage system of the Harappan civilization was the most advanced in the ancient world - more advanced than contemporary Mesopotamian cities.

Economy & Trade

  • Agriculture: Wheat, barley, rice, cotton, peas, sesame
  • Crafts: Bead-making, pottery, metalwork, seal-carving
  • Trade: Extensive trade with Mesopotamia (called "Meluha" in Mesopotamian texts)
  • Weights & Measures: Standardized system based on multiples of 16
First in the world: The Harappans were the first to cultivate cotton, which the Greeks later called "Sindon" (from Sindh).

The Great Bath - Mohenjo-daro

The Great Bath is the most famous structure of Mohenjo-daro:

  • Dimensions: 12m long, 7m wide, 2.4m deep
  • Made of baked bricks with bitumen (natural tar) for waterproofing
  • Surrounded by changing rooms and a gallery
  • Likely used for ritualistic bathing
Significance: The Great Bath indicates the importance of ritual purification in Harappan society, a practice that continues in Indian culture today.

Knowledge Check #2

What was the standardized brick ratio used in Harappan construction?
The Harappans used a standardized brick ratio of 4:2:1 (length:width:height) across all their cities, showing remarkable uniformity in construction standards.

Script & Seals

  • The Harappan script remains undeciphered
  • Written from right to left (boustrophedon style)
  • Over 4,000 seals have been discovered
  • Most common seal animal: Unicorn Bull
  • Seals were likely used for trade and identification
UPSC Important: The Harappan script is still undeciphered and is one of the most frequently asked facts in UPSC Prelims.

Religion & Beliefs

  • Mother Goddess: Numerous terracotta figurines found
  • Proto-Shiva (Pashupati): Famous seal showing a figure in yogic posture surrounded by animals
  • Nature Worship: Evidence of tree worship (Pipal tree)
  • Animal Worship: Bull was an important religious symbol
  • No Temple: No evidence of any temple has been found
Key Fact: No evidence of temples or places of worship has been found in any Harappan site, unlike contemporary civilizations of Egypt and Mesopotamia.

Burial Practices

Three types of burial practices have been found:

  1. Complete burial: The most common type (found at most sites)
  2. Fractional burial: Burial of bones after exposure (found at Mohenjo-daro)
  3. Cremation: Burial of ashes in urns (found at Lothal)

Bodies were generally buried in a north-south direction with pottery and personal ornaments.

Knowledge Check #3

Which animal is most commonly depicted on Harappan seals?
The Unicorn Bull is the most commonly depicted animal on Harappan seals. While other animals like elephant, tiger, and rhinoceros also appear, the unicorn bull is found on the majority of seals discovered.

Decline of the Civilization

Multiple theories have been proposed for the decline:

  • Climate Change: Shifting of monsoon patterns, drought
  • River Changes: Drying up of the Ghaggar-Hakra river system
  • Flooding: Repeated floods (especially at Mohenjo-daro)
  • Aryan Invasion Theory: Now largely discredited by modern scholars
  • Tectonic Activity: Earthquakes causing river diversions
Most Accepted: The decline was likely a combination of environmental factors - climate change and river drying - leading to gradual de-urbanization rather than sudden collapse.

Legacy & UPSC Relevance

  • UNESCO World Heritage Site: Mohenjo-daro (1980), Dholavira (2021)
  • Rakhigarhi excavations providing new DNA evidence about Harappan people
  • Frequently asked in both Prelims and Mains
  • Key for Ancient Indian History section of GS Paper I
Recent Development: Dholavira was inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 2021, making it an important current affairs topic for UPSC.

Final Assessment

Which Harappan site was inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 2021?
Dholavira in Gujarat was inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 2021. It is the third Harappan site to receive this status after Mohenjo-daro (1980) and the Archaeological Ruins at Moenjodaro.
